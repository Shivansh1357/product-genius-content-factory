# The most expensive page on your store is the one nobody optimizes

**Target:** Heidi Zak, CEO & Co-founder, ThirdLove (and her growth/e-commerce lead)
**Channel:** Website blog post (top of funnel, SEO + organic social)
**Source:** Product Genius technical whitepaper, founder talk, "Large Interaction Models" thesis

---

Every DTC apparel brand obsesses over the product page and the checkout. Those are the pages with dashboards pointed at them. The page almost nobody optimizes is the one in between: the moment a shopper lands, scans, and silently decides whether anything here is for them.

For an intimates brand, that moment is brutal. A woman arrives knowing her bra never fits right. She is not browsing for fun. She is auditing you. Within a few seconds she is asking a question your homepage cannot answer: "Do you have my size, my shape, and proof you understand the difference?" If the page shows her a grid of hero images and a size quiz she has to opt into, you have already lost a slice of the session. Not because the product is wrong. Because discovery is slow.

We have a number for how expensive that slowness is. Across controlled A/B tests on 25 brands and millions of sessions, the gap between a standard store and one that adapts to each shopper in real time was a 36 percent average lift in revenue per session. In apparel specifically, the lift averaged 34 percent. That is not a redesign. That is the same traffic, the same catalog, the same ad spend, converting more of the people who were already on the page.

## Why discovery is slow on most stores

The usual fix for discovery is a recommendation engine trained on historical data. It works, eventually, for shoppers who look like last quarter's shoppers. It struggles with exactly the cases that matter most to a brand like yours: the first-time visitor, the new collection, the shopper whose size or shape sits outside the fat part of the curve.

There is a deeper problem we ran into directly. When we first tested with a fast-moving fashion brand, the AI would produce a strong revenue lift for a week, then the lift would vanish within hours, on its own, with no change from us. Shopper interests had shifted. A competitor changed pricing. A new drop landed. By the time we had enough data to call the change statistically significant, the moment was gone. Tracking each traffic segment separately only made it worse, because splitting the data thinned out every signal.

The lesson reframed how we think about personalization. If you wait for statistical significance before you act, you are always optimizing for a shopper who has already left. You have to move faster than the speed of statistics.

## What "learning in minutes" actually changes

Our system is built on what we call a Large Interaction Model. The short version: instead of pulling levers after the data confirms a trend, it adjusts its recommendations for each shopper as they scroll, click, and linger, and it reinforces what works before the numbers are conclusive. In practice it learns meaningful improvements in fewer than a dozen interactions per shopper, and it keeps adapting as interests and inventory move.

Two things follow from that, and both matter for an intimates brand.

First, it does not need a giant pool of historical data to start working. The approach is effective on stores with as few as ten thousand sessions a month, which means a new collection or a newly launched size does not sit in a cold-start penalty box.

Second, it runs without third-party data. Everything it learns comes from the live, on-site interaction. For a category built on trust and body data, that is not a compliance footnote. It is part of the pitch you make to the customer.

## The shape of the fix

Picture the homepage reading the room. A shopper lands from a paid social ad for a specific style. Instead of a static grid, she sees a feed that opens with that style, then adapts as she engages: surfacing the sizes and shapes adjacent to what she touches, pulling in the fit guidance and reviews that answer the question she has not typed yet, and quietly reordering itself with every swipe. The shopper can also just tell it what she wants. It is the difference between a store that waits to be searched and a store that pays attention.

For a brand whose entire differentiation is fit, that is the most natural place in the world to apply continuous learning. The hard part of selling intimates online has always been helping someone find the right thing fast, before doubt sets in. That is a discovery problem, and discovery is exactly what gets cheaper when the page learns in real time instead of in quarters.

The product page and the checkout are mostly solved. The expensive, unoptimized middle is where the next 34 percent is sitting.
